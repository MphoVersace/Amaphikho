What is Gender-Based Violence?

Gender-Based Violence (GBV) is any act of harm directed at a person based on their gender. It can happen to anyone. Abuse is never your fault. It can take many forms:

Physical Abuse

Any act of violence like hitting, slapping, choking, or using a weapon.

Sexual Abuse

Any sexual act, contact, or comment that happens without your consent.

Emotional Abuse

Threats, insults, isolation from family, or controlling your behaviour.

Financial Abuse

Controlling your access to money, stopping you from working, or taking your money.

Know Your Rights

You have legal rights. Understanding them is a critical step towards safety and justice.

1. The Right to a Protection Order

You have the right to apply for a Protection Order (also called a "restraining order") from a Magistrate's Court under the Domestic Violence Act. This is a court order that stops your abuser from harming or threatening you.

2. The Right to Be Treated with Dignity

When you report a crime (like assault or rape) at a police station (SAPS), you have the right to be treated with respect, privacy, and dignity. You can request to be interviewed by an officer of the same gender.

3. The Right to Medical Attention

You have the right to medical treatment and examination (e.g., at a Thuthuzela Care Centre) to collect medical evidence. You can receive Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) to help prevent HIV infection after a rape.

4. The Right to Legal Aid

If you cannot afford a lawyer, you have the right to apply for free legal help from Legal Aid South Africa for criminal cases and serious civil matters (like getting a protection order).

Navigating High-Risk Areas

We cannot provide a live map of "hotspots," as they change and are specific to your area. Instead, here is how you can identify and navigate high-risk situations.

On Foot

  • Stick to well-lit, busy streets. Avoid dark alleys or deserted areas.
  • Walk with confidence and purpose. Keep your head up and be aware of your surroundings (avoid headphones).
  • Know the locations of "safe spaces" on your route (e.g., a 24-hour garage, police station).

Driving

  • Keep your doors locked and windows closed, especially when stopping at intersections (robots).
  • Park in well-lit areas. Have your keys ready *before* you get to your car.
  • If you suspect you're being followed, do not drive home. Drive to the nearest police station or busy public area.

At Night

  • Try to travel with a group. There is safety in numbers.
  • Tell a trusted friend or family member where you are going and when you expect to be back.
  • Ensure your phone is fully charged before you go out.
